Spencer Nelson Interview

Shylo and Alyssa

Why did you choose to go into this industry?

I chose this industry because I think it’s nice to see things moving in a satisfying way. Polishing and making different parts of videos look natural with motion graphics is really satisfying.

What are some of the most important skills you need to work as a motion graphics designer?

Understanding key frames and bezier curves as well as you can, because as soon as you figure out how to make those look natural, the visual stuff just kinda follows.

How did you advertise yourself to potential employers in this field?

I did it in a way where I added a bunch of stuff to my portfolio and sent that out alongside my resume. I also took the internship course and it helped me meet new potential employers for the present and future.

How do you deal with criticism, whether constructive or negative?

All criticism is welcome, even if it hurts the soul, you’re still getting like that raw side of people, and usually people’s gut reaction of “does this look wrong or bad?” is usually right, because you can tell, recognizing that uncanny valley is something that comes naturally to some people so it’s good to have those opinions.

Who is an artist that has inspired your work?

I think as far as animation goes, Felix Colgrave has probably inspired me the most. All his animations are really fluid, he uses a lot of interesting techniques, and I find that a lot of the time when I see him pull something off, I wonder how he did it, and then how I can apply it in what I'm working on.

What does your workflow look like or consist of?

Storyboarding and trying to figure out what works well and then trying to incorporate the principles of animation because if you’re missing some of those in a crucial spot it’s gonna suck, and then just kinda like staring at it and replaying it and editing over and over again until it hits right.

What pieces in your portfolio are you most proud of and why?

I would say my motion graphic advertisement for a fake prescription drug called Lactic Acid OD, just because it was a passion project driven around audio that I liked and then I concepted Ideas of stuff, and then it ended up working out in a way that didn’t feel abnormal, which was rare and nice at the time.

What is the most challenging project you’ve worked on and how did you approach those challenges?

Generally anything with a walk cycle was difficult, and I figured out a better way to do it by getting extensions to make walk cycles for me, and either using those or reverse engineering it and kinda setting the keyframes and customizing it here and there. Walk cycles suck, they suck so bad.

What’s your biggest struggle in this industry?

Understanding how much time it’s going to take, because you can factor in the art, and then you factor in “well it’s probably gonna take me this long to animate” and then it takes you two or three times as long and there’s also export times and, depending on if you get stuff more complicated, your computer chokes and then time bloats.

Would you recommend this career to other people interested in working as a Digital Designer, Video Editor, Visual Effects Artist, Video Producer or Animator?

I would say so because it’s a very versatile field, motion graphics can fit into a lot of things and all the skills that you learn while working on it can be applied to like anything in the industry, animation wise.
